# What makes a Yu-Gi-Oh! Deck GOOD?

Author: Joshua Schmidt.
Source: <https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9jiKWUaj1Kk>

## Summary

The video argues a deck's competitive strength is not a single number
but how well it covers a set of independent aspects.
The intro does not announce a fixed count (he just says "a couple of aspects"),
but he walks through 8 distinct ones:

1. **Ceiling** the raw ceiling of the combo:
   how many bodies, how many disruptions, how much attack it can put up.
   Higher is better, but raising it usually costs the other aspects.
2. **Consistency** the raw chance of opening a playable hand.
   More of a hard requirement than a bonus: shoot for over 90%,
   and remember raw one-card-starter counts overstate it.
3. **Resilience to hand traps** how well the deck plays through popular non-engine
   like Ash, Imperm, Nibiru, and Mulcharmy Fuwalos.
   Redundant normal-summon starters look consistent but fold to one interruption;
   starters that also work as extenders (Yummy) play through.
   A weakness to a specific card (Nibiru) is not a knockout, just a meta-timing call.
4. **Going second into a board** the engine's quality when breaking a board:
   reliance on the normal summon and weakness to removal (Maliss) is bad,
   engines that resolve without committing and get stronger going second (Dracotail) are good.
   Also depends on whether the format's boards lean removal versus negation.
5. **Layers on the end board** diversity of interruption sources
   (field, spells/traps, graveyard, hand) so a single board breaker
   like Dark Ruler or Raigeki can't wipe everything.
6. **Follow-up / grind game** not being out of gas by turn one,
   so you still have another strong push on turn three when your board gets broken.
7. **Non-engine space** how much interaction you can fit
   while staying consistent and powerful.
   More room also means more deck-building flexibility.
   Usually trades off against ceiling.
8. **Engine requirements** the count and, more importantly, the quality of cards
   you must run but don't want to draw.
   Fine when still usable (Dracotail Flame, Mystical Space Typhoon),
   bad when drawing one bricks or dead-cards a starter (the new Call of the Haunted archetype).

Takeaway: almost no deck fulfills all 8 (one that does is likely tier zero).
The realistic goal is to be at least average at every aspect,
good at several, and terrible at none, while picking the right meta moment
for whatever specific weakness the deck has.

### Ceiling

Ceiling is the potential of the combo the deck can reach:
how many bodies it produces, how many disruptions it ends on,
how much attack it puts up going second.
The higher the ceiling, the more likely you win when you combo off going first,
and the more likely you overwhelm the opponent going second.
Raising it comes at the cost of the other aspects.

Recent high-ceiling decks mash engines together (K9 Vanquish Soul, Yummy Mitsurugi):
a lot of starters and extenders plus powerful end-board pieces,
ending on a stack of interruptions that is hard to play through.
Ceiling is not everything though: Atlantean Mermail and Heroes both have high ceilings
but lack in other departments, so they don't see much success.
A low-ceiling deck (pure K9) trades the "unfair" end board for more non-engine.

### Consistency

Consistency is the raw chance of opening a play:
given your game plan, what are the odds you see the cards to execute it?
It is more a requirement than a bonus, because lack of ceiling you can compensate,
but lack of consistency you cannot.
A common benchmark is over 90%: a 40-card deck with 15 starters
has a 91.9% chance to open at least one (hypergeometric calculator).
Raw one-card-starter consistency overstates it though, because it ignores hand traps.
You can also just play a lot of games and get a feel for how often the deck bricks.

### Resilience to hand traps

After base consistency, the next step is how well the deck plays through
the popular non-engine in the format:
Ash Blossom & Joyous Spring, Infinite Impermanence, Mulcharmy Fuwalos,
and Nibiru, the Primal Being.
If all your starters are redundant normal summons,
one Infinite Impermanence on the summon stops you even with two or three starters in hand.
This is why Yummy works so well: every Yummy also functions as an extender,
so they play through interruption instead of passing to an Infinite Impermanence.

A weakness to a specific card like Nibiru, the Primal Being or Droll & Lock Bird is not a knockout,
it is a meta-timing call: pick the moment when that card is not popular.
At the 300th YCS, Nibiru was not popular because the main decks
(Branded, Dracotail, Radiant Typhoon) were not weak to it,
which is part of why Kamal could win with Yummy Radiant Typhoon Tri-Brigade.
Mulcharmy Fuwalos is the current gatekeeper:
Yummy has a solid compromise line under it, while Unchained has to give three to five draws
before it reaches anything like a disruption.
Board breakers (Forbidden Droplet, Lightning Storm, Super Polymerization, Evenly Matched)
phase in and out, so pick a deck that lines up with what is currently played.

### Going second into a board

Similar to playing through Ash Blossom & Joyous Spring and Infinite Impermanence:
how good is the engine when breaking a board.
Maliss is reliant on the normal summon and weak to removal,
has to climb all the way to link three, so it struggles and leans on non-engine instead.
Dracotail does not need board presence:
you activate Rahu Dracotail, Ketu Dracotail, Branded Fusion,
Dracotail Faimena, and Dracotail Mululu without committing much,
and it has in-engine removal (Dracotail Urgula, Dracotail Pan) that gets stronger going second.
It also depends on whether the format's boards lean removal or negation:
Dracotail is weak into negation (a D/D/D Wave High King Caesar every deck makes)
but strong into removal, because nothing stops its fusion spells from resolving.

### Layers on the end board

Layers means a diversity of interruption sources on the end board:
monsters on the field, spells and traps you set, graveyard effects, and cards in hand.
A layered board survives a single board breaker.
Dracotail (monsters, face-down traps, Dracotail Faimena in hand)
and K9 Vanquish Soul (monsters plus K9-X Forced Release, the Vanquish Soul trap,
Vanquish Soul Hollie Sue, and K9-17 Izuna in hand)
do not fold to one Dark Ruler No More or Raigeki,
because not all their interruption sits on the field.
The more layers, the better.

### Follow-up and grind game

Follow-up is not being out of gas by turn one.
You can't expect to combo off and auto-win on turn one:
people hand trap you and throw board breakers, and you may only make a half board.
Yu-Gi-Oh games run longer than one turn,
so you want another strong push on turn three when your board gets broken.
Not every deck needs to go infinite, but if it can't push again on turn three, that is rough.

### Non-engine space

Non-engine space is how much interaction you can fit
while keeping the deck consistent and powerful enough.
More room means more ways to interact going second (hand traps or board breakers)
and more flexibility to adapt to the format.
It usually costs ceiling: pure K9 has more room than K9 Vanquish Soul.
A deck that needs 35 engine cards can only adapt with a couple of slots,
while one that needs 15 to 20 has far more freedom with the rest.

### Engine requirements

Engine requirements are cards you must run but don't want to draw,
usually one-offs you plan to search rather than draw
(the Radiant Typhoon trap, K9-ØØ Lupis for K9-17 Izuna,
PSY-Frame Driver for PSY-Framegear Gamma).
What matters more than the count is the quality: how bad is it if you draw one.
Dracotail Flame and Mystical Space Typhoon are fine to draw:
still usable, sometimes even making your lines more flexible.
The new Call of the Haunted archetype is the bad case:
drawing Call of the Haunted makes one of your best starters a dead card
(it needs to set Call of the Haunted from deck or graveyard),
which forces you to play multiples and makes more of your hands worse.

### Conclusion

It is rare for a deck to fulfill all of these (if it does, it is probably tier zero).
The goal is to not be terrible at any of them:
at least average everywhere, good at several, and no huge weakness to common non-engine.
